Google must take 'more drastic actions' like layoffs because not enough employees will leave on their own, analyst says
Tom Dotan 4 hours ago
Sundar Pichai, CEO of Google and Alphabet, speaks on artificial intelligence during a Bruegel think tank conference in Brussels, Belgium January 20, 2020.
Sundar Pichai, CEO of Google and Alphabet. REUTERS/Yves Herman/File Photo
Google's way of slimming down through attrition won't work in the current economy, an analyst said.
Google previously avoided making cuts because it would undermine its employee-friendly reputation.
Google's headcount grew much faster than its revenue during the third quarter.
Google is one of the last major holdouts among tech giants to avoid laying off employees amid an economic slowdown. And while the company takes pride in the fact that it's never suffered mass layoffs in its history, there may be no other way out for Google, one analyst says.

The typical methods that Google would use to thin its ranks — namely, letting its employees leave for jobs at other tech companies without trying to keep them — won't work anymore, Bernstein analyst Mark Shmulik wrote in a note to clients. Venture capital funding is looking dry, which means startups won't be hiring. On top of that, companies like Meta and Amazon already made layoffs. In other words, the "natural attrition" tactic won't work.

"Google is likely going to find it difficult to reduce headcount growth below revenue growth without more drastic actions," Shmulik wrote.

In the last quarter Google hired around 12,000 new employees, making it 24% larger than it was during the quarter last year. Meanwhile its revenue only grew 6% compared to the quarter last year—prompting one activist investor, TCI, to demand the company makes major layoffs immediately.

The headcount issue came up during Google's last earnings call. CEO Sundar Pichai and CFO Ruth Porat both said they intend to significantly slow down hiring in the fourth quarter. But several analysts appeared frustrated with the fact that even in the slowed pace, the company still projected it would hire 6,000 more people.

Already, Google has tightened its performance review standards, which could put more employees on the chopping block.

"If you have a job at Google, you're keeping your head down and hoping that cutting toro from the sushi bar is the only cut that affects you," Shmulik wrote. 

Google's reluctance to conduct mass layoffs is cultural and historical. The company has built a reputation as an employee-friendly workplace and doesn't want the stigma of undergoing layoffs, several former top executives told Insider. Shmulik alludes to that in his note, pointing to Google's "distinctive culture that actively avoids loud efforts focused on cost reduction and discipline."

That has left the company bloated and slow to take decisive action, several former Google executives told Insider. While the company has previously cut individual projects like the gaming initiative Stadia it recently shut down, these have been targeted layoffs. That won't be enough with Google's profit margin shrinking as the company's ranks continue to balloon, critics say. And unlike in the past, the cost cutting won't happen by itself.

"The VC market has cooled dramatically, meaning there is no more funding to grow headcount, and the crypto market has imploded, which cuts out an entire subsector of the potential tech employment pool," Shmulik wrote.
